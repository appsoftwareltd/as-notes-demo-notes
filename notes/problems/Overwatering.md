# Overwatering

[[Overwatering]] is the number one killer of houseplants worldwide. It is also the most ironic, because it comes entirely from love. You wanted to care for your [[Plants]]. You gave it water. You gave it more water. You gave it water because it rained outside and that felt relevant. And now your [[Plant]] is dying, and it is technically your fault, but also it is the fault of the people who sold it to you without explaining any of this.

You are not alone. This happens to everyone.

## Why Overwatering Kills Plants

Roots need oxygen as well as water. When [[Soil]] stays consistently wet, the air pockets fill with water and roots can't breathe. They begin to die. From dying roots comes [[Root Rot]] — a fungal condition that spreads quickly and is very hard to reverse.

The tragedy: the symptoms of [[Overwatering]] look almost exactly like the symptoms of underwatering. [[Yellow Leaves]], wilting, general droopiness. People respond by... watering more. This is a spiral with only one ending.

## How to Tell if You're Overwatering

Before you water, **check the [[Soil]]**. Stick your finger in up to the first knuckle. If it's still damp: put the [[Watering Can]] down and step away.

Other signs:
- Soil consistently soggy for more than a week
- Yellowing lower leaves
- Mouldy smell from the pot
- Roots visible at the surface and they're brown, not white

## What To Do

1. **Stop watering immediately.** Today.
2. Check for [[Root Rot]] — gently slide the [[Plant]] out of its pot and examine the roots
3. If roots are mushy and brown, you need to act — see [[Root Rot]]
4. Move the [[Plant]] somewhere with better [[Sunlight]] and airflow to help the [[Soil]] dry out
5. If the [[Soil]] is staying wet for too long, consider [[Repotting]] into a mix with better drainage — see [[Soil]]

## Prevention

The key habit: **check before you water**. Never water on a schedule without first confirming the [[Soil]] needs it. Different [[Plants]] need water at different rates (a [[Succulent]] and a [[Monstera]] have completely different opinions about this). There is no one-size-fits-all schedule.
