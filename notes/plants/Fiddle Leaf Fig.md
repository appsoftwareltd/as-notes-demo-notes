# Fiddle Leaf Fig

*Ficus lyrata* — the [[Fiddle Leaf Fig]]. Named for the large, violin-shaped leaves that have made it the single most photographed [[Plant]] in interior design history. It sits in the corner of aspirational apartments and lifestyle blogs alike, looking magnificent, moody, and entirely aware of the fuss being made about it.

The [[Fiddle Leaf Fig]] is, it must be said, genuinely difficult. It has opinions about humidity. It has strong feelings about being moved. It will drop leaves to express its dissatisfaction in ways that feel personal. But when it's happy — and it does occasionally consent to being happy — it is breathtaking.

## Care Summary

| Factor | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Light | Bright indirect [[Sunlight]]; ideally near a large east or west-facing window |
| [[Watering]] | Every 1–2 weeks; allow top 2 inches of [[Soil]] to dry out before watering |
| [[Soil]] | Well-draining potting mix; perlite added helps significantly |
| Humidity | High; mist regularly or use a humidifier nearby |
| Feeding | [[Fertiliser]] every 2 weeks in spring/summer; none in winter |

## Light: The Non-Negotiable

The [[Fiddle Leaf Fig]] needs bright, consistent indirect light. A spot near a large window that doesn't receive harsh midday sun is ideal. Rotate the pot a quarter turn every couple of weeks so all sides get equal light — the [[Plant]] will otherwise lean dramatically toward the window, which is atmospheric but structurally problematic.

Do not move it once it is settled and happy. Moving a [[Fiddle Leaf Fig]] to a new position in the room is the fastest way to trigger a leaf-dropping sulk that can last weeks.

## Watering: The Other Non-Negotiable

[[Overwatering]] will kill it. Under-watering will also kill it, just more slowly. The correct amount of watering sits in a narrow band where the [[Soil]] is moist but never soggy, and dries out — but not completely — between sessions.

Check the [[Soil]] with your finger before watering. If the top 2 inches are still damp, wait. If they're dry, water thoroughly until it drains from the bottom. Never let it sit in standing water.

Brown spots in the middle of a leaf usually mean [[Overwatering]] or [[Root Rot]]. Brown spots at the leaf edges usually mean dryness or low humidity. The [[Plant]] will tell you which problem it has — if you know the language.

## Common Problems

- **Dropping leaves suddenly**: Usually a reaction to being moved, a cold draught, or sudden temperature change — stabilise its environment
- **Brown spots in leaf centres**: [[Overwatering]] or bacterial infection from soggy [[Soil]] — let the [[Soil]] dry, remove badly affected leaves
- **Brown, crispy leaf edges**: Low humidity or inconsistent [[Watering]] — mist more and establish a regular watering schedule
- **[[Yellow Leaves]]**: [[Overwatering]], poor drainage, or insufficient [[Sunlight]]
- **Dust on leaves**: Wipe with a damp cloth — large leaves collect dust, which blocks light and looks untidy

## Pruning and Shaping

To encourage a bushier rather than lanky shape, trim the top of the main stem during the growing season. This prompts the [[Plant]] to branch laterally. Be warned: the cut will release a white, milky sap that is a skin irritant — wear gloves or wash hands thoroughly afterward.

## Propagation

[[Fiddle Leaf Fig]] can be propagated from stem cuttings with at least one node and leaf. Allow the cut end to dry for an hour to seal the sap, then place in water or moist [[Soil]]. It is possible but slow — patience is required, and success is not guaranteed. The [[Plant]] remains difficult even at the propagation stage, which is on-brand.
