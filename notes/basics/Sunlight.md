# Sunlight

[[Plants]] need light to photosynthesise, which is the technical term for "turning sunlight into food." Without it, they go pale, leggy, and sad — much like someone who has spent too long working from home.

## Light Levels

### Direct Sunlight
South-facing windowsills in full sun. Bright, unfiltered, hot. Great for [[Succulent]] varieties and Mediterranean herbs. Will crisp other [[Plants]] fairly efficiently.

### Bright Indirect Light
Near a sunny window but not in the direct beam. The sweet spot for the majority of popular houseplants. Put your [[Monstera]] here.

### Medium Light
A few feet back from a window. Adequate for many [[Plants]] but not ideal. If your [[Plant]] starts reaching dramatically toward the nearest light source, it's been too long.

### Low Light
Far from windows, or north-facing rooms. Only a handful of [[Plants]] genuinely thrive here. [[Peace Lily]] is the classic example and will forgive you for a dim corner.

## Signs Your Plant Needs More Light

- Pale, washed-out leaves
- Long, spindly stems (called "etiolation" — a great word for Scrabble)
- Leaves smaller than they should be
- New leaves look weedy and disappointing
- The plant leans toward the window with the desperation of someone trying to catch a train

## Signs Your Plant Has Too Much Light

- Scorched, bleached, or crispy patches on leaves (especially if they're touching the glass)
- Soil drying out extremely fast, forcing constant [[Watering]]
- General crispy vibes

## Rotating Your Plants

Turn your [[Plants]] a quarter turn every few weeks so all sides get equal light. Otherwise one side goes lush and the other looks like it's given up. This is easier to do if you've established a [[Watering]] schedule — combine tasks.

## Artificial Light

Grow lights exist and work. If your home is genuinely dark, or you want to grow [[Plants]] in an interior space, a decent LED grow light will sort you out. They're not just for suspicious basements.
