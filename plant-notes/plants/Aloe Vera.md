# Aloe Vera

*Aloe barbadensis miller* — [[Aloe Vera]]. One of the most recognisable [[Plants]] on earth, found on windowsills, bathroom shelves, and tucked into medicine cabinets from here to the antipodes. Its reputation precedes it: snap a leaf, apply the gel, and the sunburn stings slightly less. Whether this is placebo or genuine relief is a matter of ongoing debate, although the plant doesn't seem particularly interested in the argument.

The [[Aloe Vera]] is a succulent, which means it stores water in its thick, fleshy leaves, tolerates significant neglect, and will die swiftly and silently if you water it too often. This is the only real risk.

## Care Summary

| Factor | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Light | Bright indirect to direct [[Sunlight]]; south or east-facing window ideal |
| [[Watering]] | Every 3–4 weeks in summer; every 6–8 weeks in winter — allow [[Soil]] to dry completely |
| [[Soil]] | Fast-draining cactus/succulent [[Soil]]; excellent drainage is essential |
| Humidity | Tolerates dry air; do not mist |
| Feeding | [[Fertiliser]] once in spring, once in summer at most; it is not greedy |

## Watering: Less Is More (and More Will Kill It)

The golden rule with [[Aloe Vera]] is to water thoroughly, then wait. Wait until the [[Soil]] is bone dry — not just surface dry, but dry all the way through. In practice, this means watering very infrequently, especially in winter when the [[Plant]] is dormant and barely uses moisture at all.

The leaves store water. When the plant needs it, it uses its reserves. Trust the system. The leaves will feel slightly less firm when it is genuinely thirsty — this is the intended signal to water.

[[Overwatering]] causes [[Root Rot]], which turns the base of the leaves mushy and brown. By the time the symptoms are visible, the damage is usually severe.

## Light

[[Aloe Vera]] wants as much light as possible. A bright windowsill with several hours of direct sun is ideal. In lower light, the [[Plant]] will stretch toward the light source and the leaves will turn a pale yellowish-green — functional but anaemic-looking.

If it has been kept in low light indoors and you want to move it outside in summer, do this gradually. Going from dim conditions to full outdoor sun causes sunscorch, which is ironic given the plant's reputation.

## The Gel

The clear gel inside [[Aloe Vera]] leaves has genuine soothing properties for minor burns, sunburn, and skin irritation. To use it, snap off an outer leaf at the base, slice it open, and apply the gel directly. Do not ingest the outer latex layer — the yellow substance between the skin and the gel is a laxative (*Aloe* latex) and should not be consumed casually.

## Common Problems

- **Mushy brown leaves at the base**: [[Root Rot]] from [[Overwatering]] — repot in dry [[Soil]], remove rotted roots, withhold water for two weeks
- **Pale yellow-green leaves**: Insufficient [[Sunlight]]; move to a brighter spot
- **Brown, dry leaf tips**: Normal on outer leaves that the plant is retiring; remove cleanly at the base
- **Leaves lying flat instead of upright**: Not enough light — the [[Plant]] is reaching

## Propagation

[[Aloe Vera]] produces offsets — small "pups" — around its base. These can be gently separated when they are a few centimetres tall and potted up individually. Allow any damaged roots to dry for a day before potting. The pups are already fully formed little [[Plants]] and will establish quickly. This is satisfying, free, and results in more [[Aloe Vera]] than you will ever need, which solves the gift problem at Christmas.
