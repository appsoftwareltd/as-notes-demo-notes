# Hoya

*Hoya carnosa* (and many other species) — the [[Hoya]], or Wax Plant. The name comes from the waxy, almost artificial-looking texture of the leaves, which gives the [[Plant]] a slightly uncanny quality, as though someone made a very convincing copy out of a material that is fractionally too shiny. This is not a criticism. The effect is distinctive and genuinely beautiful.

[[Hoya]] is a trailing and climbing vine that produces clusters of small, star-shaped flowers with an extraordinary waxy texture of their own and an unexpectedly strong, sweet fragrance. It is not in a hurry — it grows slowly, flowers on its own schedule, and rewards patience considerably more than impatience.

## Care Summary

| Factor | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Light | Bright indirect [[Sunlight]]; some direct morning sun is welcome |
| [[Watering]] | Every 1–2 weeks in summer; every 3–4 weeks in winter — allow [[Soil]] to dry between waterings |
| [[Soil]] | Well-draining mix; adding perlite or orchid bark improves drainage and aeration |
| Humidity | Appreciates higher humidity; misting or a pebble tray helps |
| Feeding | [[Fertiliser]] every 2–4 weeks during spring/summer |

## Flowering

[[Hoya]] flowers appear on short stalks called spurs, and these emerge from the same spurs repeatedly — which means you must never cut the spent flower stalks off. If you remove the spur, you remove all future flowers from that point. Let them be. They look untidy until they don't anymore.

Encouraging flowering requires bright light, slightly potbound conditions (a plant comfortable in its pot rather than one with vast acres of [[Soil]] to explore), and a cooler, drier winter period. The plant often flowers in late spring through summer.

## Trailing and Training

The vines can trail from hanging baskets, wind around a trellis, or climb a small hoop support. Left to trail, the cascading wax-leaf vines are quietly spectacular. Given a structure to climb, they will take up considerably less horizontal space and produce larger leaves.

Do not remove aerial roots that appear along the vines — these are the climbing mechanism and the plant's way of drawing in humidity. They can be guided toward [[Soil]] if you like, but they are not a problem.

## Common Problems

- **Dropping leaves or yellowing**: Usually [[Overwatering]] or cold — check [[Soil]] moisture and location
- **No flowers**: Insufficient [[Sunlight]], too much pot space, or no winter rest period — try moving to a brighter spot and reducing winter watering
- **Wrinkled leaves**: The [[Plant]] is thirsty; water thoroughly and the leaves should plump back up within a day
- **Leggy growth with small leaves**: Not enough light — move closer to a window

## Varieties

The *Hoya* genus contains hundreds of species, and the variety available has expanded dramatically in recent years:

- ***Hoya carnosa*** — the classic; green leaves, sometimes with cream or pink variegation ('Tricolor', 'Krimson Queen')
- ***Hoya kerrii*** — the heart-leaf hoya; sold as single leaves which will not grow further (save yourself; buy a full plant)
- ***Hoya australis*** — faster growing, larger leaves, vigorous climber
- ***Hoya pubicalyx*** — dark leaves, dramatic deep pink to black-red flowers
- ***Hoya linearis*** — soft, narrow leaves that cascade like green threads; unusual and beautiful

## Propagation

Take a stem cutting with 2–3 nodes, remove the lower leaves, and place in water or moist perlite until roots develop. [[Hoya]] roots readily and the cuttings establish well. Expect a wait of several months before new growth really gets going — this is normal.
