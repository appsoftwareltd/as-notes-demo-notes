# Bird of Paradise

*Strelitzia reginae* / *Strelitzia nicolai* — the [[Bird of Paradise]]. Named after the extraordinary flowers of *S. reginae*, which erupt in vivid orange and blue and look precisely like the exotic bird after which they are named. Indoors, under typical household conditions, you will mostly be growing it for the foliage — those large, paddle-shaped leaves that bring the drama and architectural presence of a tropical garden into a living room.

This is a large [[Plant]] that intends to announce itself. It will not be backgrounded. If you have the space, the light, and the patience for slow growth, it is one of the most rewarding tropical plants you can keep.

## Two Species

| | *Strelitzia reginae* | *Strelitzia nicolai* |
|---|---|---|
| Common name | Bird of Paradise | White Bird of Paradise / Giant Bird of Paradise |
| Mature size | ~1.5m indoors | Can exceed 3m indoors |
| Leaves | Smaller, paddle-shaped | Large, banana-palm-like, may split |
| Flower | Vivid orange and blue | White/blue |

*S. nicolai* is more commonly sold as a houseplant for its dramatic foliage. *S. reginae* is the classic, with better flowers but a smaller frame.

## Care Summary

| Factor | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Light | Bright light; as much direct [[Sunlight]] as possible — south-facing windows ideal |
| [[Watering]] | Every 1–2 weeks in summer; every 3–4 weeks in winter — allow top half of [[Soil]] to dry |
| [[Soil]] | Well-draining, rich potting mix; moisture-retentive but never waterlogged |
| Humidity | Appreciates humidity; tolerates average home conditions |
| Feeding | [[Fertiliser]] every 2 weeks during spring/summer |

## Light: The Priority

The [[Bird of Paradise]] needs as much light as possible. Unlike many tropical houseplants, it is not content with bright indirect light alone — it wants direct sun for at least a few hours per day. A south or south-west facing window is ideal. In insufficient light, growth becomes very slow, leaves may split even when healthy, and flowering will not occur at all.

If the large leaves develop splits, this can be normal — the long paddle-leaves split along their veins in windy conditions in the wild, reducing resistance. Indoors, splits are often caused by knocks, low humidity, or the plant adjusting to conditions. They are not necessarily a sign of anything wrong.

## Flowering Indoors

Flowering indoors is achievable but requires patience and the right conditions. *S. reginae* typically takes 3–5 years to reach flowering maturity from a young plant, requires a bright, sunny position, and benefits from being slightly potbound. Do not disturb the roots unnecessarily. The flowers, when they appear, are worth every year of waiting.

*S. nicolai* rarely flowers indoors and is grown primarily for its magnificent foliage.

## Watering

The [[Bird of Paradise]] prefers moist but well-drained [[Soil]]. Water thoroughly, then allow the top half of the [[Soil]] to dry before watering again. In summer, during active growth, it is a relatively thirsty [[Plant]]. In winter, reduce watering significantly.

Brown leaf edges can indicate either low humidity or inconsistent [[Watering]] — wipe the leaves with a damp cloth periodically to keep them clean and to increase local humidity slightly.

## Common Problems

- **Brown leaf edges**: Low humidity or inconsistent [[Watering]]; misting the leaves and establishing a regular watering routine helps
- **[[Yellow Leaves]]**: [[Overwatering]], or natural senescence of old lower leaves — check [[Soil]] moisture
- **No new leaves**: Insufficient [[Sunlight]] or underfed — increase light and restart [[Fertiliser]] schedule in spring
- **Leaves splitting**: Normal along the vein length in certain conditions; abnormal splits from rough handling or pests should be addressed
- **Root-bound and circular roots**: Can restrict growth — when [[Repotting]] into a larger pot, gently tease the root ball apart

## Repotting

The [[Bird of Paradise]] resents frequent disturbance, but a slightly potbound plant flowers more readily than one with excessive [[Soil]] around the roots. Repot only when roots are visibly escaping the drainage holes or crowding the surface — typically every 2–3 years. Choose a heavy pot; a large, top-heavy [[Bird of Paradise]] will topple a lightweight plastic container with predictable consequences.

## Propagation

Propagation is by division of the rhizome when [[Repotting]]. This is viable but not trivial — the roots are thick and interwoven and the [[Plant]] will sulk for a period after division before recovering. Seed propagation is possible but takes 3–10 years to produce a flowering plant, which tests the limits of most gardeners' patience.
