# Season Guide

[[Plants]] are not merely decorative objects. They are living organisms with an internal calendar, biological drives, and strong opinions about what month it is. Treating a [[Plant]] the same in January as in July is one of the most common mistakes new plant owners make.

Here's what to do, and when.

---

## 🌱 Spring (March–May)

Spring is the season of opportunity. [[Plants]] are waking up from winter dormancy and ready to grow. This is your busiest season.

**To do:**
- Resume [[Watering]] more frequently — check the [[Soil]] weekly
- Resume feeding with [[Fertiliser]] — monthly for most houseplants
- This is the ideal time for [[Repotting]] — [[Plants]] bounce back fastest in growing season
- Propagate cuttings — rooting is much faster in warm, bright conditions
- Check for [[Pests]] — infestations often flare up as temperatures rise
- Move [[Plants]] toward brighter windows as the sun strengthens

**Watch for:**
- New growth appearing — exciting, endorsing, confirmation you've done something right
- [[Spider Plant]] spiderettes beginning to appear

---

## ☀️ Summer (June–August)

Peak growing season. [[Plants]] are working hard. Your job is to keep up.

**To do:**
- [[Watering]] may be needed every week or more in hot, dry conditions — always check the [[Soil]] first
- Continue [[Fertiliser]] feeding — every 2–4 weeks for most [[Plants]]
- Watch for [[Pests]] — spider mites in particular love hot, dry air
- Provide shade from harsh direct afternoon sun — even [[Succulent|Succulents]] can burn against glass
- Keep windows open or a fan running — good airflow deters fungal issues

**Watch for:**
- [[Soil]] drying out much faster than normal
- Leaf scorch if [[Plants]] are touching hot window glass
- [[Monstera]] putting out new leaves at a satisfying rate

---

## 🍂 Autumn (September–November)

Transition time. [[Plants]] are slowing down. So should you.

**To do:**
- Gradually reduce [[Watering]] frequency — check [[Soil]] before every single session
- Stop feeding with [[Fertiliser]] by October at the latest
- Move [[Plants]] closer to windows as daylight shortens
- Avoid [[Repotting]] — [[Plants]] don't bounce back as easily in declining conditions
- Bring any outdoor [[Plants]] inside before the first frost

**Watch for:**
- Leaf drop from deciduous varieties — this is normal, not a crisis
- [[Yellow Leaves]] on lower, older growth — also normal, see [[Yellow Leaves]]
- A general slow-down in growth — this is what's supposed to happen

---

## ❄️ Winter (December–February)

Everyone, including your [[Plants]], is just trying to get through it.

**To do:**
- [[Watering]] infrequently — once every 2–4 weeks for most [[Plants]], even less for [[Succulent|Succulents]]
- Do NOT feed — [[Plants]] cannot process nutrients while dormant and the residue damages [[Soil]]
- Keep [[Plants]] away from cold draughts and radiators (both are bad; one is freezing, one is desiccating)
- Wipe dust from leaves — less [[Sunlight]] means every photon matters
- Accept that nothing interesting will happen until March and that's fine

**Watch for:**
- [[Overwatering]] is most common in winter — cold, wet [[Soil]] = [[Root Rot]] risk
- [[Peace Lily]] may look very sad. It is fine. Water it when very dry and wait.
- Any plant near a cold window overnight — bring it away from the glass

---

## General Rule

When in doubt: less [[Watering]] in winter, more [[Fertiliser]] in summer, and [[Repotting]] in spring. Follow those three rules and you'll be ahead of the majority of plant owners.
